CORE 104 Research Project: Analysis of 1.88 Million US Wildfires Dataset

About the Dataset

This dataset contains a spatial database of wildfires that occurred in the United States from 1992 to 2015 recordered by the US Department of Agriculture. It includes 1.88 million geo-referenced wildfire records, representing a total of 140 million acres burned during the 24-year period.

Goal

The goal of this analysis is to provide a broad overview of the dataset with a specific emphasis on differences between the US states and changes over time.

Method

I used Python for data processing and cleaning. The visualizations were creating using a combintaion of Python and D3.js. This website was created with HTML, CSS and JavaScript.

Comments on 'Part I: Differences between States'

As expected, the analysis concluded that California experiences the most wildfires out of all the US states with 189,550 fires from 1992 to 2015. This is not surprising as California wildfires have both dominated our media and our coursework. Surprisingly, Georgia and Texas trailed somewhat closely behind California with 168,867 and 142,201 wildfires experienced, respectively. These two states were not frequently referenced in our coursework, however. Montana, the site of two fires covered in our course work (the Great Fire of 1910 and the Mann Gulch fire of 1949), exhibited a surprisingly low number of around 40,000 wildfires.

In terms of fire seasons, in his Land on Fire, Gary Ferguson states that the American West experiences a “run of trouble-filled summers” (22) when it comes to wildfires. The high frequency of wildfires in the summer months is also reflected in the fires we have studied in class; the Great Fire of 1910 and the Mann Gulch fire, for instance, both occurred in August and the Yarnell Fire, in June. The dataset supports the summer months as a major fire season. We can see a peak of fire instances beginning in June and ending in October, especially in California.

Comments on 'Part I: Differences between States'

In 1910, the Forest Service declared war on wildfires. Following the devastation of the Great Fire of 1910, the Forest Service began attacking all fires “from the air and on the ground, with chemicals dropped like bombs and with bulldozers to scrape perimeter lines..growing into a force of nature” (Egan 273). Much of our coursework has argued that in doing so “the Forest Service had only fed the beast” (274). As Ferguson states, by trying to quell wildfires, the Forest Fires had only achieved the opposite: “climate change, combined with heavy fuel loads from decades of aggressive fire suppression, has led to bigger, hotter wildfires.” (40) In his Land on Fire, he cites a conservation between him and a California firefighter in illustrating this issue:

“Not that long ago, 4,000-acre fires were a big deal. But now firefighters are routinely called out on burns three or four times that size. “I think about the big fire season in California back in 1987,” says highly experienced firefighter Matt Holmstrom, superintendent of the Lewis and Clark Hotshots in Great Falls, Montana. “And then the next year, massive burns in Yellowstone. They seemed like such exceptional years. And now they’re pretty much routine.” (23)

But what does the data say? Indeed, the analysis of this dataset does seem to support this conclusion. We can see an increase in overall fires, as well as an increase in more aggressive fire classes such as E, F, and G. Contrasting purely the findings from 1992 to 2015, the number of G class (5000+ acres in damage) has increased more than 500%--from 58 to 292.The E and F classes also exhibited a significant surge, with 150% and 170% increases, respectively.
